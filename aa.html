Global Warming — Final Presentation

Prepared by: Mario Hernández

Understanding the causes, impacts, and solutions to one of the most urgent challenges facing humanity.

Overview

Global warming refers to the long-term rise in Earth's temperature due to human activities, primarily the emission of greenhouse gases like CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide.

Causes

  • Fossil Fuel Burning: The biggest source of CO₂ emissions.
  • Deforestation: Reduces carbon absorption and releases stored carbon.
  • Agriculture: Livestock emits methane; soil management emits nitrous oxide.
  • Industry: Cement, chemicals, and manufacturing processes emit greenhouse gases.

Impacts

Rising Temperatures

The last decade has recorded the highest temperatures in modern history.

Sea Level Rise

Glaciers melt and oceans expand, threatening coastal cities.

Extreme Weather

Storms, floods, and droughts are becoming more frequent and severe.

Biodiversity Loss

Ecosystems shift or collapse, putting species at greater risk.

Solutions

  • Adopt renewable energy: solar, wind, hydro.
  • Improve energy efficiency in homes and industries.
  • Protect forests and restore ecosystems.
  • Promote sustainable agriculture and responsible consumption.
  • Support climate policies at local and national levels.

Final Message

Addressing global warming requires collective action. Every individual, community, and nation plays a vital role in shaping a sustainable future for the planet.

Scientific Basis of Global Warming

Climate science relies on extensive data gathered through satellites, weather stations, ocean buoys, and paleoclimate studies. The overwhelming consensus is that human activity is the main driver of accelerated warming.

  • Greenhouse Effect Intensification: Human emissions amplify the natural heat-trapping effect.
  • Ocean Heating: Oceans absorb most excess heat, contributing to coral bleaching and altered currents.
  • Ice-Albedo Feedback: Less ice means less sunlight reflected, accelerating warming.
  • Carbon Cycle Overload: Forests and oceans can’t absorb emissions fast enough.

Social & Economic Impacts

Warming affects global systems, economies, and vulnerable populations, increasing inequality and long-term risks.

  • Health Risks: More heat-related illnesses and spread of vector-borne diseases.
  • Economic Damage: Extreme weather destroys crops, homes, and infrastructure.
  • Climate Migration: Rising seas and disasters force millions to relocate.
  • Agricultural Stress: Weather instability reduces crop yields and food security.
  • Water Scarcity: Droughts reduce freshwater availability.

Advanced Solutions & Global Policies

Effective climate action combines international agreements, technological advances, and strong local initiatives.

  • Paris Agreement: Aiming to limit warming to below 2°C.
  • Carbon Markets: Encouraging industries to reduce emissions.
  • Renewable Expansion: Solar, wind, tidal, and green hydrogen.
  • Resilient Cities: Flood defenses, green architecture, urban forests.
  • Climate-Resilient Agriculture: Drought-resistant crops & precision farming.

Future Climate Scenarios

The choices made in the next decade will determine the climate pathways for centuries.

  • Low Emissions: Rapid decarbonization keeps warming near 1.5°C.
  • Medium Emissions: Slower action leads to warming around 2–3°C.
  • High Emissions: Continued fossil fuel growth pushes warming above 4°C.

Each scenario profoundly affects ecosystems, economies, and global stability.

Scientific Data on Global Warming

Global warming is supported by an extensive body of scientific evidence gathered over decades. Multiple independent research groups continuously monitor Earth’s temperature, atmosphere, oceans, and polar ice using satellites, observational stations, and climate models.

  • Global Temperature Rise: The planet’s average surface temperature has increased by approximately 1.1°C (2°F) since the late 19th century, primarily due to increased carbon dioxide and human-made emissions.
  • Ocean Warming: Over 90% of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases is absorbed by oceans, leading to rising sea levels and disruptions in marine ecosystems.
  • CO₂ Concentration: Atmospheric carbon dioxide levels now exceed 420 ppm, the highest concentration in at least 800,000 years.
  • Glacial Retreat: Glaciers around the world—including the Andes, Alps, and Himalayas—are losing mass at unprecedented rates.
  • Sea Level Rise: Global sea levels have risen about 20 cm (8 inches) in the last century, with projections showing continued acceleration.

Key Terminology Expanded

  • Greenhouse Effect: A natural process where certain gases trap heat in Earth’s atmosphere. Human activity has intensified this effect, causing the planet to warm faster.
  • Carbon Footprint: The total amount of greenhouse gases—mainly CO₂—produced by an individual, organization, or activity, usually measured in tons.
  • Mitigation: Actions taken to reduce or prevent greenhouse gas emissions, such as transitioning to renewable energy or improving energy efficiency.
  • Adaptation: Efforts to adjust livelihoods, infrastructure, and systems to minimize harm from climate impacts, such as building flood-resistant structures.
  • Climate Models: Computer simulations that predict future climate trends based on atmospheric physics and various emission scenarios.
  • Renewable Energy: Energy sources that are naturally replenished, such as solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal power.
